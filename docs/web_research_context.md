## What Has Been Researched
- [Iter 1] Wholesale sneaker distributor margin structure and DTC channel shift risk in Europe: This iteration produced high-quality, multi-source business intelligence directly addressing all three open questions from the data analysis. The research draws on primary sources (Adidas Annual Reports 2022–2023, Foot Locker SEC filings) and credible trade/financial media to establish concrete margin benchmarks, document the DTC cycle's impact on wholesale distributors, and contextualise the company's account expansion strategy within a broader industry fragmentation trend.

On margin structure: Footwear wholesale distributors at the company's scale (~€36m revenue) operate at structurally thin gross margins estimated at 30–40%, with EBITDA margins in the low single digits, as implied by the 0.55x EV/Revenue M&A benchmark for footwear wholesalers. This compares unfavourably to DTC brand-level gross margins of 50–60%. The data analysis shows the company's Online channel is growing +143.8% for Sustaining SKUs versus Wholesale declining -18.2% — this margin differential is the financial mechanism explaining why that divergence matters beyond mere revenue mix: each euro of revenue migrating from Wholesale to Online carries materially higher contribution. The European inventory overhang documented through early 2024 adds a further near-term risk, as even stable wholesale revenue may be achieved at increasing promotional depth, compressing already thin channel economics.

On the DTC shift: Nike cut approximately 50% of its retail partnerships between 2017 and 2021, Nike Direct reached 44% of Nike brand sales by FY2023, and Adidas wholesale fell from 61% to 59% of net sales between 2022 and 2023 — all while accumulating €1.2 billion in excess inventory that was partly resolved by 'significantly reduced sell-in to the wholesale channel.' This channel withdrawal was directionally catastrophic for concentration-dependent distributors: Foot Locker, which derived 75% of revenue from Nike in 2020, saw that share fall to approximately 55% by Q4 2022, driving 460 basis point gross margin compression and pushing its EBIT margin target out to 2028. However, by 2023–2024, both Nike and Adidas have materially reversed course and are re-engaging wholesale — creating a paradox for the company under analysis. Re-engagement by major brands may crowd out the fast-fashion/emerging-brand SKUs that constitute the company's core growth engine, even as it temporarily stabilises wholesale channel health. The Fading SKU pattern (13.5% run-rate retention in Wholesale per the data analysis) is fully consistent with how wholesale accounts destock during brand pull-backs and hype-cycle collapses.

On account expansion: The company's 62.5% account base growth (1,040 to 1,690 accounts) is structurally coherent with documented fragmentation dynamics. As Nike/Adidas reduced wholesale allocations, surviving multi-brand retailers diversified aggressively — Foot Locker added Hoka (50 doors) and On Running (420 doors) — and smaller boutique operators emerged as new distribution points for second-tier and challenger brands. The company's model of supplying fast-cycle fashion sneaker brands positions it as a secondary brand aggregator filling the void left by major brand DTC withdrawal. However, this structural opportunity is fragile: the same margin compression documented at large retail chains (Foot Locker: -290 to -460 bps) cascades to smaller boutiques, and the company's long tail of 1,426 accounts generating only 28.6% of wholesale revenue faces elevated churn risk if boutique-level economics deteriorate further. The secondary market data showing the share of sneaker releases trading above retail declining from 58% in 2020 to 47% in 2024 provides a complementary signal that hype-cycle economics underpinning the fast-fashion SKU model are structurally eroding.
- [Iter 1] Fashion footwear product lifecycle and boom-bust dynamics: The web research returns a rich body of industry intelligence that directly contextualizes the most anomalous patterns identified in the data analysis. The research establishes three critical reference points. First, footwear carries the highest new product failure rate of any consumer category at 79% (First Insight, citing Gartner/Harvard/MIT), which provides an industry baseline against which to benchmark the company's observed ~93% SKU attrition by month 11. The company's attrition rate is materially worse than even this high-failure-rate industry norm, confirming that its product portfolio is weighted toward the most volatile, trend-dependent end of the footwear spectrum. Second, the 2021–2023 macro cycle was historically extreme in both directions: the global sneaker market spiked 19.5% in 2021, then decelerated to 2.7% growth in 2022, with the industry entering a slower-than-average growth phase from mid-2023 onward per Bank of America analysis. This cycle directly explains the company's extraordinary new SKU revenue contribution (54.2% from SKUs with no FY21 history) — the FY22 vintage of new SKUs launched into a market with exceptional tailwinds, and the subsequent deceleration creates a material forward risk. Third, the wholesale channel is structurally under pressure across the industry, with leading brands shifting aggressively toward DTC, fragmenting the multi-brand retailer landscape. This is highly relevant given the company's reliance on ~1,626 wholesale clients, of whom 1,426 represent the long-tail. The research also provides a Spain-specific market anchor: the Spanish sports sneaker market is valued at approximately €2.8 billion in 2024, growing at a projected 4.5% annually through 2027 — a moderate growth rate that does not support continued double-digit revenue expansion through market growth alone. The overall synthesis confirms that the company's fast-churn SKU model is directionally consistent with industry fast-fashion footwear norms, but the quantitative severity of its SKU attrition and the thinness of its stable SKU base (only 8) represent elevated structural risk at precisely the moment when the macro cycle has shifted from tailwind to headwind.
